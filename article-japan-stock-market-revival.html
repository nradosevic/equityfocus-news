Japan's Stock Market Revival: Why Global Investors Are Returning to Tokyo

By Kenji Watanabe • March 8, 2026

After decades of false starts and broken promises, Japan's stock market appears to be experiencing a genuine renaissance. The Nikkei 225 has surged to levels not seen since the bubble era of the late 1980s, but unlike that speculative frenzy, today's rally is underpinned by substantive corporate reforms, improved profitability metrics, and renewed foreign investor interest. For global asset allocators who have long underweighted Japan, the question is whether this recovery has further room to run—or whether decades of conditioning to "fade Japan rallies" remains the prudent approach.

The most significant driver of changed sentiment is corporate governance reform. Beginning with Prime Minister Abe's initiatives and accelerating through subsequent administrations, Japan has systematically pushed companies to prioritize shareholder returns. The Tokyo Stock Exchange's explicit pressure on companies trading below book value to articulate capital efficiency plans has produced tangible results. Record levels of share buybacks, dividend increases, and strategic divestitures of non-core assets signal that Japanese management teams are finally responding to shareholder concerns that were long dismissed as inappropriate foreign interference.

Warren Buffett's well-publicized investments in Japanese trading houses served as a powerful catalyst, providing what some called "permission" for institutional allocators to follow. When the world's most respected value investor declared Japanese equities attractive, it challenged the prevailing narrative that Japan was a value trap rather than a value opportunity. Since Buffett's initial disclosures, foreign ownership of Japanese equities has increased markedly, reversing years of net outflows.

Currency dynamics add complexity to the investment case. The yen's weakness against the dollar has boosted the earnings and competitiveness of Japan's export-oriented companies while making Japanese assets cheaper in dollar terms for foreign buyers. However, it also means that dollar-based investors have not fully captured the local currency gains in index levels. Some investors view yen weakness as an additional opportunity, anticipating eventual currency normalization. Others hedge currency exposure, accepting the cost to isolate the underlying equity returns.

Beyond governance and currency, structural factors support the bull case. Japan's manufacturing excellence in semiconductors, industrial automation, and precision components positions the country to benefit from ongoing global capital expenditure in AI infrastructure, factory automation, and supply chain diversification from China. Japanese companies often possess deep technical moats and long-standing customer relationships that create durable competitive advantages less visible in simple valuation screens.

Skeptics raise legitimate concerns. Japan's demographic challenges—an aging and shrinking population—create long-term headwinds for domestic consumption and labor availability. The government's massive debt burden, while largely domestically held, constrains fiscal flexibility. And despite progress, corporate governance improvements remain uneven, with some companies embracing shareholder capitalism while others resist. Historical experience teaches humility: Japan has appeared "finally turning around" multiple times before disappointing.

For portfolio construction, the question is not whether Japan deserves inclusion but at what weight. After years of underallocation, many global portfolios have minimal Japanese exposure despite the country representing a significant share of developed market capitalization. Gradual increases to benchmark or above-benchmark weights—combined with active selection to favor companies demonstrating genuine governance improvement—may offer a balanced approach to capturing potential upside while managing the risks that have historically punished Japan bulls.
