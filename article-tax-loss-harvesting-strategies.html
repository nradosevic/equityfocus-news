Tax-Loss Harvesting: Strategies That Actually Work for Different Account Types

By Christine Park, CPA • February 27, 2026

Tax-loss harvesting has evolved from an obscure wealth management technique to a heavily marketed feature of robo-advisors and brokerage platforms. The basic premise is straightforward: sell investments trading at a loss to realize capital losses that can offset capital gains or ordinary income, then maintain market exposure by purchasing similar (but not "substantially identical") securities. When executed properly, tax-loss harvesting can meaningfully improve after-tax returns over time. However, the devil resides firmly in the details, and many investors either over-complicate or under-optimize their approach.

The first critical consideration is which accounts benefit from tax-loss harvesting. Taxable brokerage accounts are the primary arena where the strategy adds value—gains and losses are recognized annually and affect current tax liability. Tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs and 401(k)s, by contrast, grow tax-deferred regardless of internal trading activity. Harvesting losses in retirement accounts provides no immediate tax benefit and may actually be counterproductive if it reduces long-term appreciation potential. Investors should focus their harvesting efforts exclusively on taxable accounts.

The wash sale rule represents the most important compliance constraint. Selling a security at a loss and repurchasing substantially identical securities within 30 days before or after the sale disallows the loss for tax purposes. This 61-day window (30 days before plus the sale date plus 30 days after) requires careful attention. The rule applies across all accounts controlled by the taxpayer, meaning that buying in an IRA what you just sold at a loss in a taxable account can trigger a wash sale. Spousal accounts are also considered for wash sale purposes.

Selecting appropriate replacement securities requires balancing tax efficiency against tracking error. Replacing one S&P 500 ETF with another S&P 500 ETF likely violates wash sale rules given their substantial similarity. However, replacing an S&P 500 ETF with a total market ETF or a large-cap growth ETF maintains similar market exposure while being sufficiently different to avoid wash sale treatment. The goal is maintaining desired asset allocation characteristics while ensuring the replacement is not substantially identical to what was sold.

Timing and frequency decisions affect harvesting effectiveness. Some investors harvest systematically on a set schedule; others harvest opportunistically when market declines create larger loss positions. More frequent monitoring generally captures more harvesting opportunities, but transaction costs and complexity increase. For most investors, quarterly or even annual harvesting reviews capture the majority of available benefits without excessive activity. Major market declines present particularly attractive harvesting opportunities that warrant out-of-cycle action.

The long-term implications of tax-loss harvesting deserve attention. Harvesting current losses reduces cost basis, meaning that eventual sales may generate larger gains. The strategy essentially converts current tax savings into future tax liability. This tradeoff is generally favorable because of the time value of money and the possibility that future gains may be taxed at lower rates, offset by other losses, or even eliminated through step-up at death. However, investors should understand they are deferring rather than eliminating taxation in most scenarios.

Finally, tax-loss harvesting should integrate with broader financial planning. The $3,000 annual limit on deducting net capital losses against ordinary income means that excessive loss harvesting in years without offsetting gains produces diminishing returns. Coordinating harvesting with anticipated capital gains from property sales, stock option exercises, or portfolio rebalancing maximizes the strategy's benefit. Working with a qualified tax advisor ensures that harvesting decisions align with individual circumstances and don't inadvertently create complications in other areas of tax planning.
