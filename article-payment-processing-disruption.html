The Next Wave of Payment Processing Disruption Is Coming From Unexpected Places

By Vanessa Reyes • March 10, 2026

For decades, the payment processing industry operated as a relatively stable oligopoly, with major card networks, large banks, and established processors controlling the flow of trillions of dollars in transactions. The first fintech wave brought companies like Stripe and Square, which simplified merchant onboarding and developer integration but largely worked within existing infrastructure. Now, a second wave of disruption is emerging from unexpected sources—software platforms, retail giants, and even social media companies—that threatens to fundamentally reshape who captures value in payment flows.

The most significant trend is the rise of software-led payments. Vertical software companies serving specific industries—from restaurant management to healthcare billing to construction project management—are increasingly embedding payment processing directly into their platforms. Rather than simply integrating with third-party processors, these companies are becoming payments facilitators themselves, capturing interchange economics that previously flowed to traditional acquirers. When a small restaurant uses an all-in-one platform for scheduling, inventory, and payments, the software company captures not only subscription revenue but also a percentage of every transaction.

This shift has profound implications for established players. Traditional merchant acquirers who relied on direct relationships with businesses find themselves increasingly intermediated by software platforms. The battleground has moved from signing merchants to signing software companies. Processors that can offer compelling embedded payments solutions—including white-label capabilities, instant onboarding, and flexible payout options—are winning distribution partnerships worth millions of transactions. Those slow to adapt risk becoming commoditized infrastructure providers with compressed margins.

Meanwhile, large retail and technology platforms are building proprietary payment networks that bypass traditional rails entirely. Major e-commerce marketplaces have launched their own payment methods that settle transactions outside card networks, using ACH or direct account connections at dramatically lower costs. Social media platforms are enabling peer-to-peer payments and in-app commerce that capture user attention and transaction data while avoiding traditional processing fees. These closed-loop systems don't eliminate cards but reduce their centrality in the payment ecosystem.

Real-time payment infrastructure is another disruptive force. The Federal Reserve's FedNow service, combined with private RTP networks, enables instant account-to-account transfers that compete directly with card payments for certain use cases. Merchants frustrated with card acceptance costs—particularly in industries like insurance, healthcare, and utilities—are exploring real-time payment acceptance that offers lower fees and immediate settlement. While consumer adoption of non-card payments remains nascent, the infrastructure foundation is now in place for acceleration.

For investors, this evolving landscape presents both risks and opportunities. Established payment companies trading at premium multiples face legitimate threats to their competitive moats. However, companies positioning themselves as infrastructure enablers—providing the APIs, compliance frameworks, and settlement capabilities that software platforms need—may actually benefit from the proliferation of payment facilitators. The key is identifying which companies are building irreplaceable capabilities versus those merely riding temporary distribution advantages.

Understanding payment economics requires examining multiple layers: network fees, interchange, processor margins, platform takes, and merchant residuals. As value capture shifts across these layers, winners and losers will emerge. The companies best positioned for the next decade of payments will be those combining technical excellence with regulatory expertise and distribution leverage—a challenging combination that favors neither pure incumbents nor pure disruptors, but rather those capable of integrating all three elements.
