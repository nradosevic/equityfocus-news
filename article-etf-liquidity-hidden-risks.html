ETF Liquidity: The Hidden Risks Passive Investors Overlook

By Dr. Natasha Goldman • March 4, 2026

Exchange-traded funds have revolutionized investing, offering low-cost diversification and seemingly infinite liquidity through real-time trading on major exchanges. Assets in ETFs globally now exceed $12 trillion, with inflows continuing to accelerate as investors embrace passive strategies. Yet beneath the smooth surface of ETF trading lies a complex liquidity mechanism that can behave unexpectedly during market stress. Understanding these dynamics is essential for investors who may need to sell at precisely the moments when liquidity becomes most precious.

The fundamental promise of ETFs relies on the arbitrage mechanism: authorized participants who can create and redeem ETF shares by exchanging baskets of underlying securities ensure that ETF prices stay close to net asset value. When an ETF trades at a premium, authorized participants create new shares by delivering underlying securities, pocketing the spread. When it trades at a discount, they redeem shares, receiving the underlying basket and profiting from the difference. This continuous arbitrage normally keeps ETF prices tightly aligned with fundamental value.

However, the arbitrage mechanism depends on the liquidity of underlying holdings. ETFs holding highly liquid securities like large-cap US stocks maintain tight spreads even during volatility because the underlying securities can be easily bought and sold. But ETFs holding less liquid assets—high-yield bonds, emerging market securities, thinly traded small caps—face potential disconnects. When underlying markets become stressed or illiquid, authorized participants widen spreads or step back entirely, causing ETF prices to diverge significantly from stated net asset values.

The March 2020 market turmoil provided a vivid demonstration. Fixed income ETFs traded at substantial discounts to their net asset values for extended periods as corporate bond markets seized up. Investors selling during this period received prices materially below what the underlying bonds were supposedly worth. While markets eventually normalized, the episode revealed that ETF liquidity is ultimately bounded by the liquidity of what the fund holds—a reality that many investors had not fully internalized.

Time-of-day trading patterns add another dimension to ETF liquidity risk. Market opens and closes often exhibit wider spreads and higher volatility as market makers adjust positions. Trading during the middle of the day when underlying markets are fully functioning typically produces better execution. Additionally, ETFs that invest in overseas markets face challenges when US trading hours don't align with local market hours—an ETF tracking Japanese stocks may show different behavior during Asian trading hours versus when Tokyo is closed.

Product structure also matters significantly. Some ETFs use synthetic replication, gaining exposure through derivatives rather than holding underlying securities directly. While this can enable exposure to otherwise inaccessible markets, it introduces counterparty risk and potential tracking errors during stress. Leveraged and inverse ETFs, designed for daily rebalancing, can produce unexpected returns over longer holding periods due to volatility decay and compounding effects that work against investors in choppy markets.

For prudent investors, these considerations don't invalidate the ETF structure but rather inform its appropriate use. Large, liquid ETFs tracking major indices remain excellent vehicles for most purposes. However, investors should scrutinize trading volumes, bid-ask spreads, and underlying asset liquidity before committing to more specialized products. Building positions gradually rather than executing large orders instantaneously, avoiding market opens and closes, and maintaining awareness of underlying market conditions all contribute to better outcomes. The ETF revolution has genuinely democratized investing—but like any powerful tool, it rewards understanding of its mechanics and limitations.
