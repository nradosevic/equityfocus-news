The Art of Corporate Debt Restructuring: Lessons from Recent High-Profile Cases

By Victoria Ashworth • March 10, 2026

The past eighteen months have witnessed a surge in corporate debt restructuring activity, driven by the collision of higher interest rates with overleveraged balance sheets accumulated during the era of cheap money. For investors in distressed credit and special situations, these restructurings offer both cautionary tales and valuable playbooks. Understanding the mechanics, stakeholder dynamics, and typical outcomes has become essential knowledge for anyone operating in leveraged credit markets.

The anatomy of a modern restructuring differs substantially from those of previous cycles. Today's corporate capital structures are more complex, often involving multiple layers of secured debt, first and second lien facilities, unsecured bonds, and various hybrid instruments. The rise of direct lending has added private credit providers to the mix of stakeholders, alongside traditional bank lenders and public bondholders. These diverse creditor bases create both complications and opportunities during restructuring negotiations.

Recent high-profile cases illustrate several recurring patterns. First, the importance of capital structure positioning: senior secured creditors consistently achieve better recoveries than subordinated claimants, but the magnitude of this advantage varies considerably based on asset quality and enterprise value. In asset-heavy businesses with tangible collateral, senior secured creditors have recovered 80-90 cents on the dollar even in severe distress scenarios. In asset-light businesses, the secured versus unsecured distinction matters less, and recoveries depend more on going-concern value.

Second, the process itself has evolved. Out-of-court restructurings, known as liability management transactions, have become increasingly sophisticated. Companies now routinely execute debt exchanges, uptier transactions, and drop-down financings that transfer value between creditor classes without formal bankruptcy proceedings. These transactions have sparked litigation and led to new documentary protections in credit agreements, but they remain a crucial tool in the restructuring toolkit. Investors must understand both the offensive and defensive implications of these techniques.

Third, timing matters enormously. The recoveries achieved by creditors who purchase distressed debt at the right moment—typically after initial panic selling but before restructuring terms crystallize—can be substantial. Conversely, investors who hold through restructuring processes without actively participating in negotiations often find themselves disadvantaged. The emergence of creditor cooperation agreements and ad hoc groups has formalized the need for active engagement in complex situations.

Fourth, sector considerations play a significant role. Retail and commercial real estate restructurings have followed different playbooks than technology or healthcare situations. In sectors with substantial ongoing operations, preserving going-concern value through debtor-in-possession financing and careful management incentives has proven critical. In sectors facing secular decline, liquidation analyses become more relevant, and the race to maximize asset values takes precedence over operational considerations.

For investors looking to participate in distressed credit, several principles emerge from recent experience. Due diligence on capital structure positioning and documentation is essential—the specific covenants and definitions in credit agreements can determine outcomes. Building relationships with other creditors and understanding their motivations helps predict restructuring dynamics. Maintaining flexibility to add to positions or reduce exposure as situations develop allows investors to adapt to changing circumstances. Finally, patience is often rewarded, as restructuring processes typically take longer than initially anticipated but can ultimately deliver attractive returns for those who navigate them successfully.
