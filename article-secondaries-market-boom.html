The Secondaries Market Boom: Why Investors Are Trading Private Equity Stakes

By Christopher Hartley • March 8, 2026

The secondary market for private equity interests has transformed from a niche corner of alternative investments into a mainstream asset class commanding hundreds of billions of dollars annually. Transaction volumes have grown at double-digit rates for most of the past decade, and 2026 appears set to establish new records. This maturation reflects fundamental changes in how investors think about liquidity in private markets and has created compelling opportunities for both sellers seeking flexibility and buyers looking for differentiated exposure.

Understanding why the secondaries market has grown requires examining both supply and demand dynamics. On the supply side, institutional investors increasingly view their private equity portfolios through a dynamic asset allocation lens rather than as static, hold-to-maturity investments. Pension funds, endowments, and sovereign wealth funds routinely rebalance their alternative investment exposures, selling positions in some funds while committing to others. Corporate M&A activity has created forced sellers as acquiring companies divest their targets' private equity holdings. And the denominator effect—where falling public market valuations increase the relative weight of private investments—has periodically pushed institutions to sell private assets to restore target allocations.

On the demand side, dedicated secondary fund managers have raised massive pools of capital specifically to purchase these interests. The appeal is compelling: secondary buyers acquire mature portfolios with known assets, shorter holding periods, and reduced blind pool risk compared to primary fund commitments. Historical returns in secondaries have been attractive, with the best managers consistently generating net IRRs in the mid-to-high teens while exhibiting lower loss rates than primary fund investments. The J-curve effect that plagues primary fund investments is mitigated or eliminated in secondaries, as buyers step into portfolios already generating distributions.

The market has also become considerably more sophisticated in its transaction structures. Traditional LP interest purchases remain the largest segment, but GP-led transactions have grown dramatically. In these deals, general partners offer their limited partners options to sell positions in continuation vehicles while rolling their own economics forward. Single-asset secondaries allow GPs to extend their hold on particularly attractive portfolio companies while providing liquidity to LPs who prefer to exit. Strip sales enable sellers to dispose of portions of their portfolios while retaining exposure to their highest-conviction positions.

Pricing dynamics in the secondary market provide important signals for the broader private equity ecosystem. During periods of market stress, secondary discounts to NAV can widen significantly, offering patient buyers attractive entry points. During bull markets, premium pricing for sought-after interests is not uncommon. Currently, pricing stands at modest discounts to NAV for high-quality buyout funds, while venture capital secondaries trade at steeper discounts reflecting the valuation uncertainty in that segment.

For institutional investors considering secondaries allocation, several strategic considerations apply. Direct secondary fund commitments offer access to experienced managers with demonstrated sourcing and underwriting capabilities. Co-investment alongside secondary funds allows for portfolio customization and reduced fee drag. Building internal secondary capabilities—while resource-intensive—can provide the greatest control and fee efficiency for the largest institutions. The appropriate approach depends on the institution's scale, governance structure, and alternative investment expertise.

Looking ahead, the secondaries market appears positioned for continued growth. The pool of private equity assets continues to expand as primary fundraising remains robust. The need for portfolio management tools and liquidity solutions shows no signs of abating. Regulatory changes have generally been supportive of secondary market development. And the professionalization of the market—with standardized documentation, established intermediaries, and deeper buyer bases—has reduced friction costs for all participants. For investors seeking private equity exposure with differentiated risk-return characteristics, secondaries merit serious consideration as a core allocation within alternatives portfolios.
